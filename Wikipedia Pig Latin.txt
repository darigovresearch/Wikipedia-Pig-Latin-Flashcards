What is Pig Latin?|<b>Pig Latin</b> (or, in Pig Latin, "Igpay Atinlay") is a <a href="/wiki/Language_game" title="Language game">language game</a> or <a href="/wiki/Argot" class="mw-redirect" title="Argot">argot</a> in which <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> words are altered. 
How is Pig Latin generated?|Usually by adding a fabricated <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">suffix</a> or by moving the <a href="/wiki/Syllable#Onset" title="Syllable">onset</a> or initial <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">consonant</a> or <a href="/wiki/Consonant_cluster" title="Consonant cluster">consonant cluster</a> of a word to the end of the word and adding a <a href="/wiki/Vocalic" class="mw-redirect" title="Vocalic">vocalic</a> <a href="/wiki/Syllable" title="Syllable">syllable</a> to create such a suffix.

What is the objective of Pig Latin?|The objective is to <a href="/wiki/Obfuscation" title="Obfuscation">conceal the words</a> from others not familiar with the rules. 
Why does Pig Latin reference Latin?|The reference to <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> is a deliberate <a href="/wiki/Misnomer" title="Misnomer">misnomer</a>; Pig Latin is simply a form of <a href="/wiki/Argot" class="mw-redirect" title="Argot">argot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cant_(language)" title="Cant (language)">cant</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Jargon" title="Jargon">jargon</a> unrelated to Latin, and the name is used for its English connotations as a strange and foreign-sounding language. It is most often used by young children as a fun way to confuse people unfamiliar with Pig Latin.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
